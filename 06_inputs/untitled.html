Final Prototype Iteration I

When our team reconvened, we spent time sketching our final design. Pictured below are several sketches of our first idea.

Based on these sketches, we began 3D printing several pieces. The first part we printed was an L bracket, which would be secured to the servo. This bracket was designed to hold the camera and connect to the front wheels. We positioned the camera at the front to capture potential obstacles, while the front wheels were linked to the servo to control turning. The motor-powered rear wheels would then follow the rotation of the front wheels.

The .stl file for the L bracket is pictured below:

The next parts we 3D printed were the gearbox, gears, and the front and back axles. Initially, we planned to position the motor behind the servo to save space. However, we discovered that the motor would interfere with the servo, limiting its rotation. As a result, we decided to house and secure the motor separately. We designed a gearbox to enclose the motor and turn the axle. Additionally, we created a connector on the gearbox to attach it to the servo compartment, making the setup resemble a train.

The .stl file for the gear box, gears, and axles is pictured below:

Final Prototype Iteration I in Action

After printing all the components, we began assembling our first final prototype iteration. During the first assembly, however, we realized that the dimensions of our prints were too precise, leaving very little tolerance. As a result, we had to reprint the axles, making them smaller to fit properly within the holes. Once the sizing was corrected and all the pieces were secured, we conducted a proof-of-concept test to ensure the pipe robot moved and turned as expected. The test was successful!

Here is a video of the forward movement!

Here is a video demonstrating the successful turning!

PROBLEM!

Sadly, our design wasn't perfect. We realized that it was too wide to fit within the pipe. To address this, we needed to completely redesign the gearbox to reduce its width. This involved raising the gearbox to allow the wheels to be placed directly underneath, rather than on the sides, which helped minimize the overall width. We then began 3D modeling a new gearbox for the second iteration of our final prototype.
